# PBL — Project-Based Learning

Students learn by working over an extended period on a complex, authentic project that answers a driving question and ends in a public product. Buck Institute (now PBLWorks) "High Quality PBL" is the modern canonical version.

## Essential elements

1. **Driving question** — open, compelling, tied to standards
2. **Sustained inquiry** — days or weeks, not a single class period
3. **Authenticity** — real-world context, real tools, real audience
4. **Student voice & choice** — students shape the product and process
5. **Reflection** — on content AND on process; ongoing, not just at end
6. **Critique & revision** — structured peer and teacher feedback mid-project
7. **Public product** — shared beyond the teacher; real audience

## How it shapes a lesson plan

- Daily plans live inside a project arc; the unit arc is the meaningful unit, not the day
- Every week includes workshops/mini-lessons on targeted skills as students need them
- Formative checkpoints replace bell-schedule quizzes
- Standards coverage is verified across the project, not per-lesson

## Language signals

- "Driving question: …"
- "Public audience: …"
- "Milestone: [checkpoint] by [date]"
- "Critique protocol: [structure]"
- "Reflection: what worked, what would you change, what do you still need?"

## Common pitfalls

- Dessert projects (a project after the unit) instead of project-based learning
- A driving question that's closed or trivia
- No critique/revision cycles → students ship first drafts
- Public product is "presentation to the teacher"
