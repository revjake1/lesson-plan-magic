# Hattie — Visible Learning

John Hattie's meta-meta-analysis of ~1,600 studies, ranking influences on student achievement by effect size (d). "Visible Learning" = both students seeing their learning AND teachers seeing their impact.

## High-impact influences (selected, d > 0.6)

- **Teacher clarity** (d ~ 0.75) — students know WHAT they're learning, WHY, and what success looks like
- **Feedback** (d ~ 0.70) — specific, timely, tied to the success criteria; also teacher getting feedback from students
- **Metacognitive strategies** (d ~ 0.55, but higher in recent work) — students think about their thinking
- **Self-reported grades / student expectations** (d ~ 1.3) — setting and monitoring toward goals
- **Reciprocal teaching** (d ~ 0.75)
- **Classroom discussion** (d ~ 0.82) — when structured
- **Direct Instruction** (d ~ 0.60) when properly implemented (see `direct-instruction.md`)
- **Formative evaluation of lessons** — teachers changing instruction based on student data

Average effect size across all influences is ~0.40 — "the hinge point." Anything below that may be worse than average teaching.

## How it shapes a lesson plan

- Learning intentions and success criteria are explicit and student-facing
- Feedback is planned into the lesson, not left to happen by chance
- Students reflect on their own learning (metacognition) as a routine, not an extra
- The teacher has a formative-evidence plan for each lesson

## Language signals

- "Learning intention: ___ / Success criteria: ___"
- "Students self-assess against the rubric"
- "Teacher feedback protocol: [structure]"
- "Students explain their thinking out loud"

## Common pitfalls

- Treating effect sizes as a ranking to implement top-down (context matters)
- Confusing Hattie's "Direct Instruction" with teacher-centered lecture
- Posting learning intentions once and never referencing them again
