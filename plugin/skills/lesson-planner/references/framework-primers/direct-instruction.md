# Direct Instruction

Explicit, teacher-led instruction with clear objectives, modeling, and frequent checks for understanding. Madeline Hunter's 7-step model is the canonical template; Engelmann's scripted DI is the purist version.

## Hunter's 7 steps

1. **Anticipatory set** — hook; activate prior knowledge
2. **Objective + purpose** — state what students will learn and why it matters
3. **Input** — new information delivered (lecture, text, video, demo)
4. **Modeling** — teacher demonstrates the skill
5. **Check for understanding** — verify students are tracking (cold-call, pair-share, whiteboards)
6. **Guided practice** — students try with teacher support
7. **Independent practice** — students work alone

A closure step (reflection, quick write) often appears after #7 but isn't in the original seven.

## How it shapes a lesson plan

- The objective is front and center; tied to a specific observable behavior
- Teacher models BEFORE asking students to do
- Checks for understanding happen 3-5 times per lesson, not just at the end
- Practice is deliberate, skill-focused, and scaffolded

## Language signals

- "Today you will be able to ___"
- "I will model… watch for…"
- "Show me on a scale of 1-5…"
- "First, we'll work through three together. Then you'll try five on your own."

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping modeling ("I already showed them last week")
- One check for understanding at the end → the lesson is already over
- Mistaking lecture for Direct Instruction — DI requires modeling and checks, not just talking

## Authoritative source

Madeline Hunter — *Mastery Teaching* (ASCD). Engelmann — Association for Direct Instruction, nifdi.org.
