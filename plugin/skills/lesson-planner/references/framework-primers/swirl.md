# SWIRL

> Speaking, Writing, Interacting, Reading, Listening — a literacy-across-content-
> areas framework adopted by many districts (notably in Georgia) to embed the
> five language modalities in every lesson, not just ELA.

## Overview

SWIRL is not a single-inventor framework; it grew out of content-area literacy
research in the 1990s-2000s and was codified in various district/state
initiatives. The premise: students build literacy fastest when every class —
science, social studies, math, arts, CTE — asks them to produce language in
all five modes, not just read textbooks and listen to teachers.

A SWIRL-aligned lesson plan explicitly tags *when* each modality occurs. A
teacher who plans 60 minutes of "lecture + worksheet" hasn't touched Speaking,
Writing, or Interacting. SWIRL surfaces that gap at plan time rather than at
evaluation time.

## Core components

1. **Speaking** — students articulate ideas aloud (to teacher, partner, group, class)
2. **Writing** — students produce written output (notes, claims, analysis, reflection)
3. **Interacting** — structured peer exchange (pair-share, debate, protocol-based discussion)
4. **Reading** — students decode and make meaning from text (not teacher paraphrase)
5. **Listening** — structured reception (peer ideas, audio, video, teacher talk with a task attached)

## How it shapes a lesson plan

- **Required:** each daily plan names at least 3 of the 5 modalities by activity
- **Emphasized:** Speaking + Interacting + Writing (the "productive" modes) should outweigh Listening + Reading (the "receptive" modes) across a week
- **Restructured:** "worksheet" is not a SWIRL activity unless it includes a writing task that demands claim-evidence-reasoning or similar

## Language signals

- "Think-Pair-Share" (Interacting + Speaking)
- "Gallery walk" (Reading + Speaking)
- "Turn and talk" (Speaking + Listening)
- "3-2-1 reflection" (Writing)
- "Annotate the text" (Reading + Writing)
- "Exit ticket claim" (Writing)
- Explicit tags like "(S)" or "(W)" next to agenda items

## Common pitfalls (for the LLM)

- **Pitfall 1:** treating every activity as all five modes. "Group work" is not automatically all of SWIRL.
- **Pitfall 2:** confusing Listening (structured, task-attached) with passive teacher-talk time.
- **Pitfall 3:** skipping Speaking entirely when the content is "quiet" (math, independent reading, tests).
- **Pitfall 4:** inventing a sixth mode. There are five. Don't add one.

## Authoritative source

SWIRL is widely used but not owned by a single publisher. Cite from a

## Notes

- In elementary self-contained classrooms, SWIRL naturally spans the day, not a single period.
- On a weekly plan, aim for all five modes across the week with emphasis on the three productive modes (S, W, I).
